Explore Gilgit Cuisine

Gilgit-Baltistan, a mountainous region in Pakistan, is known for its unique and flavorful cuisine, which utilizes local ingredients and traditional cooking methods. Popular dishes include Prapu (noodle dish), Mamtu (dumplings), and Balay (noodle soup). The region also boasts a variety of fruits like apricots, cherries, and mulberries, which are often incorporated into dishes or enjoyed dried.

Harisa

History: Harissa is a traditional Gilgit-Baltistan dish, a slow-cooked stew primarily made with wheat, meat (usually chicken or lamb), and spices. It's not a daily meal but is prepared for special occasions like festivals, celebrations, and funerals. The dish is known for its rich, thick consistency and unique flavor, achieved through slow cooking.

Ingredients: made with a combination of ingredients including wheat, meat (often mutton or chicken), and various spices, slow-cooked to a porridge-like consistency.

Recipe:Harisa is a warm dish made by cooking wheat and meat together slowly. Wheat is soaked overnight and boiled until it becomes soft. Boiled meat is shredded and mixed into the wheat while cooking. The mixture is stirred on low flame with ghee, salt, and black pepper. It is served hot, often topped with fried onions and extra ghee.

Chapshuro

History: Chapshuro, also known as Chhap Shoro, is a traditional meat-filled flatbread dish from the Gilgit-Baltistan region of Pakistan, particularly popular in Hunza and Nagar. It's often referred to as the "Hunza pizza" and is a hearty staple enjoyed during celebrations and everyday meals. While its exact origins are unclear, it's believed to have originated in nearby Thole Nagar and has become a symbol of Hunza hospitality, often served to welcome visitors.

Ingredients: The key ingredients include minced meat (beef or mutton), onions, green chilies, ginger, garlic, and various spices, all encased in a wheat flour dough.

Recipe: Prepare dough using flour, water, and a pinch of salt. Mix minced meat with chopped onions, tomatoes, green chilies, salt, and pepper. Roll out the dough, place the filling inside, and seal it like a flat round pie. Cook on a griddle or bake until both sides are golden and fully cooked. Serve hot with chutney or yogurt or tea for a traditional Gilgit flavor.

Gyaling

History: Gyaling, also known as Go-Lee or Gral, is a traditional Hunza Valley dish, specifically a thin, stretchy wheat bread often served with butter and hot tea, particularly during celebrations of the wheat harvest. It's a staple in Hunza, known for its simplicity and rich, buttery flavor, often enjoyed with namkeen or herbal tea.

Ingredients: made with simple ingredients like whole wheat flour and water, sometimes with the addition of salt, and is cooked on a skillet or griddle. It's often enjoyed with butter, honey, or apricot jam.

Recipe: Mix wheat flour with water and a pinch of salt to make a soft dough. Roll the dough into thin round flatbreads using a rolling pin. Cook the flatbreads on a hot griddle (tawa) until both sides are lightly browned. Brush with melted butter or ghee while still hot for extra flavor.

Apricot Soup

History: Apricot soup, known as Bataring Daudo in the Hunza region of Gilgit-Baltistan, is a centuries-old dish deeply rooted in the local culture and cuisine. It's a simple yet nutritious soup, traditionally made with dried apricots, water, and sometimes a touch of brown sugar. The soup is a staple, especially during the winter months, and is known for its warming and fortifying properties.

Ingredients: made with dried apricots, water, flour, and sometimes sugar or lemon. It's a popular winter remedy known for its potential health benefits like relieving cold symptoms and constipation.

Recipe: Soak dried apricots in water for a few hours until they soften. Boil the soaked apricots with a pinch of salt until they become mushy. Mash the apricots and simmer the mixture to form a thick soup. Add a little wheat flour or barley flour for thickness, stirring well. Serve warm as a sweet and tangy soup, often eaten in winter for energy.
