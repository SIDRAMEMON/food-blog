Explore Punjabi Cuisine

Punjabi cuisine, originating from the Punjab region in India and Pakistan, is known for its rich, flavorful dishes, both vegetarian and non-vegetarian, with a strong emphasis on dairy and wheat products. It's a cuisine deeply rooted in the region's agricultural lifestyle, utilizing fresh produce, lentils, and grains. A distinctive feature is the use of the tandoor (clay oven) for dishes like tandoori chicken, naan, and kebabs.

Paya

History: Paya, a slow cooked stew made with lamb or goat trotters, has its roots in the kitchens of South Asia, particularly in the Indian subcontinent. It's believed to have originated in the Mughal courts, where chefs perfected the art of utilizing less expensive cuts like trotters to create flavorful dishes. Over time, paya became a popular breakfast staple in regions like Punjab

Ingredients:The primary ingredient for Paye, a traditional Punjabi dish, is mutton or beef trotters (feet), also known as paya. Other essential ingredients include a blend of aromatic spices, ginger, garlic, onions, and sometimes yogurt or tomatoes.

Recipe: To make a traditional and flavorful paye dish, start by thoroughly cleaning the trotters (paye) with warm water and a little vinegar or flour to remove any odor or residue. In a large pot, heat oil and sauté finely sliced onions until they turn golden brown. Add ginger-garlic paste and cook until the raw smell disappears. Then add the cleaned trotters and fry them well with the masala for several minutes to seal in the flavor. Stir in whisked yogurt along with turmeric, red chili powder, salt, and optional garam masala. Mix everything thoroughly and add enough water to cover the trotters. Cover the pot and let it simmer on very low heat for several hours—traditionally 4 to 6 hours—or until the trotters become tender and release their natural gelatin, creating a rich, sticky gravy. Alternatively, you can use a pressure cooker for faster results. Once done, garnish with freshly chopped coriander and thin ginger slices.

Makki di Roti & Sarson da Saag

History:Makki di roti and sarson da saag is a traditional Punjabi dish with deep historical roots, particularly within the Punjab region of India and Pakistan. The dish, consisting of corn flatbread and mustard greens, is believed to have originated over 2,500 years ago. It's a staple in Punjabi cuisine and is often associated with the region's agricultural heritage and the hardworking lifestyle of its people.

Ingredients: Makki di Roti and Sarson ka Saag is a classic Punjabi dish. The main ingredients for Sarson ka Saag (mustard greens) are mustard greens (sarson), spinach (palak), and bathua (a type of leafy green). For Makki di Roti, the primary ingredient is maize flour (makki ka atta)

Recipe: Boil mustard, spinach, and bathua leaves until soft, then blend into a thick paste. Sauté garlic, ginger, and green chilies in ghee, add the greens, and simmer with maize flour. For the roti, knead maize flour with warm water and shape flatbreads by hand. Cook each roti on a hot tawa with ghee until golden and crispy.

Murgh Cholay

History: Murgh Cholay, a popular Punjabi dish of chicken and chickpeas, has a history intertwined with legends and culinary evolution. While the exact origin story is debated, the dish's popularity is undeniable, particularly in the Pakistani and North Indian regions. It's often associated with the Mughal era and a story involving Emperor Shah Jahan.

Ingredients:Key ingredients include chicken, chickpeas, onions, tomatoes, ginger-garlic paste, and a blend of spices like coriander, turmeric, red chili powder, and garam masala.

Recipe:To prepare a hearty and flavorful chicken chana masala, begin by heating oil in a deep pan and sautéing finely chopped onions until golden brown. Add crushed ginger and garlic, and cook until fragrant. Stir in chopped tomatoes and cook until soft and the oil separates. Add chicken pieces and cook them on medium heat until they turn white and begin to release their juices. Once the chicken is partially cooked, add boiled chickpeas and mix well. Sprinkle in traditional spices like cumin powder, coriander powder, red chili, turmeric, salt, and a pinch of garam masala. Allow everything to simmer gently so the flavors blend together beautifully. To enhance richness, add a few tablespoons of whisked yogurt and cook until the gravy thickens and coats the chicken and chickpeas evenly. Finally, garnish with freshly chopped coriander, green chilies, and a squeeze of lemon juice.

Nihari

History: Nihari, while a popular dish across South Asia, is not a Punjabi dish in origin. It is a stew that originated in Lucknow, India, during the Mughal era. While nihari is enjoyed in Punjab and sometimes considered a Pakistani national dish

Ingredients: Nihari, a flavorful stew, typically includes meat (beef shank is common), bones with marrow, and a blend of aromatic spices. Key spices include long pepper, black cardamom, green cardamom, cloves, cumin, coriander, fennel, and bay leaves. Other ingredients often include ginger, garlic, onions, wheat flour (for thickening), and various chili powders.

Recipe: Cook beef or mutton with bones slowly in water and special Nihari spices.Fry onions, garlic, and ginger, then add the meat and mix well.Add a little wheatflour mixed with water to make the curry thick.Let it cook on low heat until the meat is soft and full of flavor.Garnish with green chilies, ginger, and lemon, and enjoy with naan or roti.
